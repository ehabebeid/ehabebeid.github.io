Palazzo dei Conservatori

The Palazzo dei Conservatori (English: Palace of the Conservators or was built in the mid-fifteenth century to house the elected Roman magistrates (called 'Conservators') (Ackerman 144). Like older structures on the Hill, notably il Palazzo del Senatore, the Palace of the Conservators's language was mostly medieval (Krautheimer 196-7). It did, however, have a portico during the medieval period, as it does now (Ackerman 155). The Palace is built partially on top of the ruins of the Temple of Jupiter.

Michaelangelo used elements of the pre-existing Palace to inform his design of the new façades for all three palaces. For dei Conservatori, he created a more monumental façade through combining colossal pilasters of the Corinthian order spanning two floors with normally-sized inventive Ionic columns on the ground floor, framing the portico (Ackerman 159). Work on the Palace lasted from the late 1530's until 1568.

Unlike the Palazzo Senatorio, the Conservators Palace had more structural concerns that Michelangelo had to attend to (Ackerman 159). As such, some of the same elements appear in all three palaces, but are only functional in one or two of them. Most notably, the stability of the Conservator's Palace's portico informed the pilaster piers' giant size, and they also doubled to partition the guild offices inside the palace; as such they were not primarily decorative (Ackerman 156).

The Palazzo dei Conservatori, the Palazzo Nuovo and the Tabularium are all part of the Musei Capitolini.
