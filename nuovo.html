Palazzo Nuovo

The Palazzo Nuovo mirrors Palazzo dei Conservatori and is one of the buildings of the Musei Capitolini. As its name (literally new palace) suggests, the Palace was built with no specific programmatic use in mind (Ackerman 146). In fact, though Michelangelo was operating under budget restrictions, he conceived of this new building for a purely aesthetic function: the completion of the new piazza's symmetry (Ackerman 145). Taking existing conditions into consideration, Michelangelo sought to mirror the preexisting angle at which Palazzo dei Conservatori is placed in relation to Palazzo Senatorio. Their respective axes intersected at 80° (Ackerman 147). In fact, this irregularity is what informs the Piazza del Campidoglio's trapezoidal design. Irregularity did not only appear there. The positioning of the Basilica of Santa Maria in Aracoeli restricted Palazzo Nuovo's potential expansion. On the other hand, Palazzo Nuovo closed the Basilica's access to the urban space in front of the Senatorial Palace.

Seeing as its purpose was primarily aesthetic, the Palace was planned simply as a façade with a portico mirroring that of the Conservators', behind which there would be some offices. The restrictions imposed by the Basilica were immaterial, since the creation of the urban space was the most important.

Work occured on this Palace long after Michelangelo's death. Its funding was not available until under Clement VIII at the turn of the century, and it was finished only in the mid-seventeenth century under Innocent X. The modern palace, unlike Michelangelo's proposal (the 'offices behind the portico'), has a court, which is in fact a seventeeth century interpolation of the plan.
